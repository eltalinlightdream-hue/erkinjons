The Development of Plastics

The first synthetic plastic was invented in 1907 by Leo Baekeland, a Belgian-born American chemist. He had been looking for a substitute for shellac, a natural electrical insulator, to meet the needs of the rapidly growing electrical industry. Baekeland produced a liquid mixture of the chemicals formaldehyde and phenol, subjected it to heat and pressure in a container he called a Bakelizer, and a new solid material was formed. The material, called Bakelite, was the world's first synthetic plastic.

Bakelite was a good insulator, it was durable, heat-resistant and could be moulded into various shapes, making it ideal for use in the electrical industry. It was soon being used for electrical switches and parts for radios and telephones. Jewellery and toys were also made from it. It was even used in early aircraft construction.

Before the invention of Bakelite, photographic film was made from nitrocellulose (a.k.a. celluloid). Celluloid was invented in 1868 as a substitute for ivory in the manufacture of billiard balls. It was flammable and could be unstable, but it was an extremely useful material for photographic film.

After Bakelite, many more new plastics were developed. Polystyrene was first produced commercially in Germany in 1931, and later developed into expanded polystyrene (EPS) foam which was first manufactured in 1954 in Britain. This kind of foam is white and very lightweight and can be moulded into different shapes. It is used extensively in packaging products such as television sets, computers and hi-fi equipment because of its protective properties. It is also used for cheap disposable cups for hot drinks.

Nylon was developed by DuPont, an American chemicals manufacturer, in the 1930s. The first product it was used in commercially was toothbrush bristles. Before this, toothbrush bristles were made from animal hair. Nylon could be extruded into fibres and was very hard-wearing. It went on to be used in surgical sutures (stitching), ropes and also in women's stockings, giving them the familiar name "nylons".

Polyethylene (polythene) is the most commonly manufactured plastic worldwide. It was first manufactured in Britain in 1933. It was invented by accident – the scientists were conducting an experiment with ethylene gas at high pressure when they noticed a waxy substance had formed in their apparatus. Polythene is used to make countless everyday items including plastic bags, drink bottles, and food packaging.

After World War II, the chemical industry began large-scale commercial production of plastics. In the 1950s, new materials such as polyester (used in clothing), Lycra, and polypropylene (used in packaging and textiles) were developed. Polyester is now the most manufactured fibre in the world, overtaking cotton.

However, the growth of the plastics industry has not been without problems. Most plastics are not biodegradable, meaning they do not break down naturally in the environment. Much plastic ends up in landfill sites or in the ocean, where it can break down into tiny pieces called microplastics, which are toxic to wildlife and can enter the food chain. Governments and companies are working on solutions, including fireproof alternatives, better recycling systems, and developing new biodegradable plastics made from glass or plant materials. Expanded polystyrene foam is one material being replaced in some countries due to its environmental impact.
